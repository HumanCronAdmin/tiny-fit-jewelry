Satomi Kawakita: Gold rings for hands that need size 2

Updated April 14, 2026

The first time I tried on a ring in US size 4, the band sat loose enough to spin on my finger. US 3? Still too big. US 2 made me cry—not from joy, but from the sudden absence of space between my knuckle and the metal. For years, that gap was all I knew: jewelry sized for someone else’s hand, where the smallest available meant “close enough.”

Satomi Kawakita changes that. Her rings start at US size 2, and they’re made for hands that shouldn’t have to compromise. No stretching, no sliding, no resigning yourself to “fine” when perfect is possible.

What This Brand Offers

US ring sizes from 2 to 4. 18K gold only. Price range: $200 to $2,000. Handmade in New York by a Japanese jeweler trained across two countries. No adjustable bands, no resizing options after purchase—just the exact measurement you ask for.

Why it matters from a petite perspective

The problem isn’t just finding a size. It’s finding a design that doesn’t look ridiculous once it fits. Most fine jewelry marketed as “delicate” still assumes average-sized fingers. A 0.5mm band on a US 6 hand can look elegant; on a US 2, it can vanish. Satomi’s rings are scaled down from the first millimeter. The proportions feel intentional, not shrunken. That’s not something a brand can photograph. You have to try it on to believe it.

Custom sizing isn’t a gimmick here. It’s the entire point. The website won’t tell you how nerve-wracking it is to email a stranger your exact finger measurement and hope the ring arrives looking like the photo. The relief when it does is worth the vulnerability.

What the community quietly says

Petite wearers note that the smallest sizes don’t feel “too tiny.” They feel purpose-built. Others mention that the organic, hammered textures add grip, so a size 2 doesn’t spin the way cheaper jewelry does. Some say the price stings at first, but the lack of resizing fees over time makes it a wash compared to buying multiple “cheap” pieces that never fit right.

Practical fit caveats

If you’re used to wearing jewelry sized for average hands, a US 2 will feel snug at first. Give it a week. The metal molds to your skin. Also, 18K gold shows scratches faster than platinum or palladium. Not a dealbreaker—just expect a patina to develop, the way vintage jewelry does.

Ask for the millimeter measurement of the band in addition to the ring size. Some designers cut corners on the smallest sizes; Satomi’s bands maintain thickness even at US 2.

Recommended pieces

Engagement and wedding rings top the list because milestone pieces should never require a “close enough” fit. Custom sizing from US 2 ensures the band won’t spin on your finger during the ceremony or slip off while you’re washing dishes years later.

Stacking rings are another standout. The delicate, often textured designs were made to layer, but their petite sizing prevents the cluster from overwhelming smaller fingers. A US 2 base ring with two stacked US 3 rings won’t look like a choker of metal—it’ll look intentional.

Who this is for (and who should look elsewhere)

If your ring size is US 2 or 3 and you want fine jewelry that doesn’t scream “costume,” this brand fits. It’s also for those willing to pay upfront for a perfect fit instead of gambling on resizing later. If you’re outside that size range or prefer silver, platinum, or non-custom pieces, keep searching.

For everyone else in between—US 3.5 to 4—this brand’s designs still offer a proportional elegance, but the value proposition changes. A custom size 2 is rare; a custom size 4 is less so.

Bottom line

Satomi Kawakita doesn’t just make small rings. She makes rings that feel like they were always meant to exist on petite hands. The price reflects the labor of hand-fabricating metal for sizes most jewelers ignore. If you’ve spent your life settling, this is the brand that stops the settling.
